Faster releases at Qorvo - IoT semiconductor R&D (Qorvo · 2024-2025)

Challenge: Every software release took three weeks to get from "ready" to "in customers' hands." In a market where speed of integration is a competitive edge, that lag held customers up - and meant senior R&D engineers spent too much time waiting or context-switching instead of building.

Approach: I ran a workshop with the development, test, and customer-integration teams to map the full path from code-complete to customer delivery - something no one had pieced together before. The map exposed three drags on the process: unclear handovers between teams, manual checks that could be automated, and quality issues being caught too late. I worked with the technical and team leads to redesign the handover points, automate the repetitive checks, and move testing earlier so defects surfaced in hours rather than days.

Result: Release cycle dropped from three weeks to two - a 33% improvement. Customers got new versions faster. Engineers spent more time on real development. And the change stuck: the new process kept running after I moved on.

Stakeholder Map - Bringing visibility to a complex IoT delivery network (Qorvo · 2024)

Challenge: Delivery on the IoT smart home SDK programme involved a wide and uneven mix of stakeholders - internal engineering teams, ODMs, customers, and external standards bodies (CSA Matter alliance, Zigbee updates from Ubisys). Influence, dependencies, and communication channels were all tangled. Some decisions needed five approvals; others were being made in side conversations. Without a shared picture of who mattered where, leadership had limited visibility into how delivery actually flowed across the ecosystem.

Approach: Built and maintained a stakeholder map with three concentric layers - internal stakeholders (home users, engineers, DevOps, internal dev/marketing/sales/QA teams), external stakeholders (customers, ODM partners), and public stakeholders (Matter standard alliance, CSA, Zigbee standard providers). Anchored the map to a specific use case - delivering an IoT smart home SDK in compliance with Matter and Zigbee - so it stayed grounded in real delivery decisions rather than becoming an abstract org chart.

Result:The map became an alignment tool used across engineering, product, and leadership. From it, the programme team identified five concrete patterns:

  • Visibility gaps for leadership (gained systemic view)
  • Flow interruptions affecting customer delivery
  • Bottlenecks without clear ownership
  • Weak feedback loops, unclear dependencies, and over-approval slowing decisions
  • Opportunities for continuous improvement (Kaizen)

Each insight fed into downstream work - bug triage redesign, governance model design, and team agreements documented elsewhere on this page. The stakeholder map wasn't the deliverable; it was the diagnostic that made the rest of the work possible.

Designing Intake Workflows - Defect Triage and Change Request Governance (Qorvo · 2024–2025)

Challenge: Two very different kinds of work were hitting the team through the same informal door. Customer-reported defects were getting buried alongside scope change requests, and both were quietly absorbing sprint capacity that had been promised elsewhere. The team needed two different front doors, because defects and scope changes are fundamentally different problems:

  • Defects are reactive - how urgent, who fixes it, when does it ship.
  • Change requests are proactive - do we take it on, and what does it displace.

Treating them the same meant customer issues sat too long, scope crept silently, and teams paid the price in slipped commitments.

Approach: Designed two parallel intake workflows, each tailored to the decision it needed to support.

  1. Bug Triage Process (co-created with Product Owners and Engineering Managers): A structured triage workflow with clear prioritisation criteria, ownership, and communication channels - integrated into regular Agile ceremonies so customer issues had real-time visibility instead of waiting on email threads.
  2. Change Request Process (co-created with the PMO): A formal review path for scope changes before they reached team backlogs: raised → PMO review (impact, risk, priority) → approval decision → team assigned → delivery plan → closure lessons logged. RAG status tracked throughout.

Result: Two clean front doors instead of one chaotic one.

  • On the defect side: response time dropped ~30%, customer trust improved through timely coordinated resolution.
  • On the scope side: teams were protected from informal scope creep, and stakeholders had a clear path for raising changes with clear feedback on what happened next.

The bigger win was conceptual. "Is this a defect or a change request?" became a routine framing question - and the right process took it from there.

Problem-solving and Prioritization Techniques in Practice (Qorvo · 2024-2025)

Across two years coaching cross-functional IoT teams, recurring patterns kept surfacing - slipping sprint commitments, defects leaking to customers, backlogs that didn't match business priorities. Rather than relying on instinct, I introduced structured problem-solving techniques to make the team's thinking visible and the decisions accountable. Below are the techniques I used most, the problems I applied them to, and what changed.

🔍5 Whys - Surfacing the real causes of missed sprint commitments

Challenge: The team was missing sprint commitments three sprints in a row. Surface explanations ranged from "too much work" to "unclear requirements," but nothing was sticking.

Approach: Facilitated a structured 5 Whys session in retrospective. The group started with "why did we miss the commitment?" and iterated until we landed on a process gap - late dependency clarification with the hardware team, not effort estimation.

Result: Reframed the problem from "team velocity" to "upstream dependency timing." Built a pre-sprint dependency check-in with hardware leads, which became part of standard refinement. Sprint predictability stabilised in the following two sprints.

🧩 Ishikawa (Fishbone) Diagrams - Mapping complex quality problems

Challenge: Recurring quality issues - defects escaping into customer releases - that the team couldn't trace to a single owner. Symptoms touched people, process, tooling, and environment all at once.

Approach: Ran two Ishikawa workshops with cross-functional teams (dev, QA, product). The first mapped contributors to low delivery predictability across five categories. The second tackled quality-bar misses in customer releases.

Result: Visualising contributors as a shared map shifted the team out of blame and into systems thinking. From the first session: identified inadequate scoping and lack of insights upstream - leading to the bug triage process redesign documented elsewhere on this page. From the second: surfaced a gap in validation effort and short cycle pressure that fed into the dev-QA team agreement work.

🎯 Pareto Rule (80/20) - Backlog prioritisation under release pressure

Challenge: Heading into a release with a long backlog and shrinking time, the team was spreading effort thinly and risking missing the critical-path features.

Approach: Facilitated a Pareto analysis with Product Owner and engineering leads - mapping which 20% of items would deliver 80% of customer value, and which blockers were causing 80% of delivery friction.

Result: Cut backlog noise, refocused the sprint on six high-impact items, and gave stakeholders a clear data-backed rationale for what was deferred. Released on time with the high-value features intact.

⏱️ Eisenhower Matrix - Cutting context-switching in a reactive delivery cycle

Challenge: The team was caught in a reactive loop - customer escalations, urgent hotfixes, and "just-one-more-thing" requests were crowding out strategic work the roadmap actually depended on. Context-switching was high, and engineers were finishing weeks feeling busy but not productive.

Approach: Introduced the Eisenhower Matrix in a working session with the team and senior stakeholders - mapping incoming work across two axes: urgent vs. not urgent and important vs. not important. Each item got placed in one of the four quadrants, with explicit decisions on what to do, schedule, delegate, or drop.

Result: Made the cost of "urgent but not important" work visible to leadership for the first time - items that had been silently consuming team capacity. Protected dedicated time for the strategic "important, not urgent" quadrant where roadmap features and tech debt actually live. Reduced mid-sprint context switching and shifted the team toward proactive planning rather than reactive firefighting.

🧭 MoSCoW -Aligning Product and Engineering on scope

Challenge: Product Owner and engineering team had different mental models of what "must" ship in the upcoming PI - leading to scope creep mid-sprint.

Approach: Ran a MoSCoW classification workshop pre-PI Planning. Each backlog item was classified as Must / Should / Could / Won't with both sides in the room.

Result: Created a shared, transparent scope baseline that held through the PI. Reduced mid-sprint scope changes and gave the team explicit permission to deprioritise "Could-haves" without negotiation overhead.

Team Manifestos & Working Agreements - From single-team alignment to cross-team handoffs (Qorvo · 2025)

Challenge: Two related but different problems surfaced across the IoT delivery teams.

Inside the development team, day-to-day collaboration norms were implicit - what "done" meant, how to handle blockers, how to run sprint planning, what the team valued in code reviews. New joiners absorbed this slowly through osmosis, which made onboarding inconsistent and accountability fuzzy.

Across the Dev-QA boundary, the friction was different. Quality standards weren't written down, handoffs created rework, and Dev and QA had subtly different mental models of what "shippable" looked like. The result: defects leaking to customers, inconsistent test coverage, and a slow build-up of "I thought you were doing that" moments.

Both problems needed the same kind of intervention - a co-created agreement - but at different scopes.

Approach: Facilitated two manifesto workshops, scoped to the problem each one was solving.

  1. Development Team Canvas (2025): Ran a team canvas workshop with the development team to make implicit working agreements explicit. The canvas covered roles, goals, values, action points, strengths and assets, weaknesses, scrum meeting norms, definition of done, code review practices, and task estimation approaches. Every cell was filled by the team, not by me - co-creation was the point.
  2. Dev-QA Cross-Team Manifesto (2024): Co-created a shared way-of-working between Development and QA, anchored on a shared goal ("Quality Assurance - test it then ship it"). Mapped inputs, outputs, training areas, process definitions, and explicit ownership of action items between SM, PO, QA Lead, and Dev team. Defined feedback loops so issues caught downstream had a clear path back upstream.

Result: The Dev Team Canvas gave the team a shared reference document - "this is how we work" - that new joiners could read on day one and contribute to in retrospectives. Implicit norms became visible and improvable.

The Dev-QA Manifesto closed the handoff gap. Quality standards became shared rather than negotiated case-by-case. Friction during handoffs dropped, test coverage conversations became proactive instead of reactive, and the two teams started operating with one mindset toward delivery.

The pattern across both: working agreements only stick when teams build them themselves. My role wasn't to write the rules - it was to facilitate the conversation, hold the structure, and make sure what came out belonged to the team.

Building a Metrics Stack for Data-Driven Delivery (Qorvo · 2025)

Challenge: Decisions about delivery were being made on instinct rather than evidence. Was the team really getting faster, or just feeling busier? Was bug volume actually going down, or were we just talking about it less? Was scope creeping into sprints in a way we could measure, or only after-the-fact in retrospectives? Without consistent metrics, retrospectives were opinion-driven, sprint commitments were optimistic, and stakeholder conversations relied on stories instead of evidence.

Approach: Built a layered metrics stack that gave the team three different lenses on delivery - each answering a different question.

  1. Sprint Burn-Up Charts (Progress lens - "are we on track?") Implemented sprint burn-up charts to visualise completed work against changing scope in real time. Unlike burn-down charts, burn-ups make scope changes visible - so when work crept in mid-sprint, the team could see it immediately rather than discovering it at sprint review.
  2. Flow Metrics Dashboard (System lens - "where is the work getting stuck?") Coached the team on flow velocity (throughput), cycle time, WIP, and flow efficiency - visualised across PIs. Tracked bugs and stories separately to surface how much capacity was being absorbed by defect work versus feature delivery, with trend lines showing whether flow was improving over time.
  3. Bug Dashboard (Quality lens - "is quality improving?") Built a consolidated bug dashboard tracking issues created vs. resolved over rolling two-week windows, with open bug trend lines and filters by product line (BLE/Matter), priority, and customer visibility. Made defect leakage visible across multiple sprints so retrospectives could act on patterns, not anecdotes.

Result: Three lenses, one shared evidence base.

The team stopped arguing about whether things were getting better and started looking at the data. Sprint commitments became more reliable because burn-ups exposed scope creep early. Bug retros became sharper because the team could see whether they were making progress on quality, not just talking about it. Stakeholder updates became data-led - "throughput is trending up, bug ratio down" replaced "things are going well."

The deeper shift was cultural: metrics moved from being something a Scrum Master reports to being something the team owned and used. Once the dashboards were in their hands, retrospectives became more honest, planning became more grounded, and continuous improvement stopped being a slogan.

Governance Model - Bringing structure to cross-team IoT delivery (Qorvo · 2024-2025)

Challenge: Delivery on the IoT SDK programme spanned multiple workstreams - software, HW/MAC, QA - each with their own cadences, owners, and escalation patterns. Decisions were being made informally across Slack threads and ad-hoc meetings, important risks were surfacing late, and there was no consistent forum for stakeholders to align on milestone progress.

Approach: Co-designed a four-layer governance model with the Programme Manager and Engineering leads, mapping decision rights and review cadences across the organisation:

  • L1 - General Management Steering Committee: strategic decisions, quarterly review
  • L2 - Programme Board: RAG reporting, risk escalation, milestone governance, monthly review
  • L3 - Workstream level: SW, HW/MAC, and QA workstream owners
  • L4 - Team level: SW Team 1 (IoT SDK App Layer), SW Team 2 (Matter Standard), HW/MAC team, QA team

A clear escalation path connected the bottom up, so blockers at team level had a defined route to leadership without needing political navigation.

Result: Created a shared mental model that engineering, product, and leadership all referenced. Reduced ad-hoc escalations into senior leadership, gave the Programme Board a consistent monthly forum with proper RAG reporting, and made delivery accountability visible - every layer knew what it owned and what it didn't.
